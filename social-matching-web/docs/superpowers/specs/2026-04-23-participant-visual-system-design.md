# Participant Visual System Pass - Warm, Dense, Calm

**Date:** 2026-04-23  
**Status:** Proposed  
**Scope:** Participant-facing journey across `/events`, `/events/:eventId`, and `/events/:eventId/apply`  
**Audience:** Product, design, engineering, ops

---

## 1. Why this pass exists

We have already improved the browse structure on `/events`.

That work made the page denser, clearer, and more useful for scanning. But it did **not** yet fully turn the participant journey into one coherent visual system.

This pass exists because the current experience still feels slightly split:

- browse is stronger, but still not fully “the same world” as detail and apply
- buttons, cards, and screen transitions do not yet share one distinct Circles language
- the inspirations have been used as direction, but not yet fully translated into the product itself

This design pass is about making the journey feel like one designed atmosphere, not a sequence of separate screens.

---

## 2. What we are trying to achieve

The participant experience should feel:

- warm
- calm
- premium
- socially alive
- softly layered
- rounded and tactile
- confident, not rigid

In practical terms, the product should feel like a curated social space, not a generic web app.

This means:

- the same emotional tone across browse, detail, and apply
- buttons that feel specific to Circles, not default UI controls
- cards and panels that feel related
- interactions that feel gentle and intentional
- enough density to be useful, but not enough to feel crowded

---

## 3. Recommended direction

### Recommended approach: hybrid participant system pass

The right next step is not a full redesign of every surface at once.

It is a **hybrid pass**:

- define the visual language once
- apply it to the most visible participant surfaces
- then refine the interaction moments that make the system feel alive

This is the best balance because it is:

- strong enough to make the inspirations real
- focused enough to avoid boiling the ocean
- broad enough to connect the whole participant journey

### Why this is better than isolated tweaks

If we keep polishing one screen at a time, we will keep getting partial improvement without full coherence.

That usually results in:

- one screen feeling elegant
- another feeling generic
- buttons that don’t match the cards
- motion that doesn’t match the tone

This pass is meant to fix that by making the whole journey share one visual grammar.

---

## 4. What should stay from the current direction

We should keep the parts that are already working.

### Keep

- the B2 dense browse direction
- attendee circles as visible social proof
- warm Hebrew-first copy
- the calm premium tone
- the idea that `/events` is a shelf, not a feed
- the browse → detail → apply progression

### Keep from the inspirations

- corner softness
- layered cards
- soft depth
- rounded interactions
- tactile surfaces
- calm motion

These are not decorative extras. They are the feeling we want the product to carry.

---

## 5. What should change

The remaining gap is not structure alone. It is the **visual and interaction language**.

We should improve:

- button style and hierarchy
- card and panel treatment
- spacing rhythm between surfaces
- how hover / press / focus feel
- how the three participant screens relate to each other
- how the page breathes on mobile versus desktop

This is what will make the inspirations feel implemented rather than referenced.

---

## 6. Design principles

### 6.1 Soft depth

Surfaces should feel layered and tactile.

The goal is not heavy shadow or dramatic float. The goal is a quiet sense of depth that makes the page feel composed.

### 6.2 Rounded confidence

Corners and interactions should feel warm and approachable.

The system should feel human and easy to enter, not sharp or overly formal.

### 6.3 Calm hierarchy

The page should always tell the eye where to go first.

The order should generally feel like:

1. title
2. context
3. social proof
4. action

### 6.4 Social presence

People should feel that other people are already in the room.

That means social proof is not an optional decoration. It is part of the participant story.

### 6.5 One family across screens

Browse, detail, and apply should feel like different depths of the same product.

Not different products.

---

## 7. Screen roles

### 7.1 `/events` as the shelf

This is the place for scanning.

It should feel compact, calm, and inventory-rich. The user should quickly understand:

- what is available
- what seems relevant
- what feels socially alive

The cards should do the heavy lifting here.

### 7.2 `/events/:eventId` as the room

This is the place for confidence.

It should feel a little richer than browse, but not different in character. The user should feel the same brand, just with more context and breathing room.

### 7.3 `/events/:eventId/apply` as the threshold

This is the place for commitment.

It should feel calm, reassuring, and clear. The user should understand exactly what happens next, without the page becoming severe or bureaucratic.

---

## 8. Buttons and actions

Buttons are a key part of whether the product feels designed or default.

### Button goals

Buttons should feel:

- rounded
- calm
- specific
- confident
- easy to spot without shouting

### Button rules

- primary actions should feel warm and intentional
- secondary actions should be present but quiet
- destructive or cautionary actions should be obviously different
- the button language should feel like it belongs to Circles, not a generic UI kit

### Button tone

Primary actions should not sound like a checkout funnel or a marketplace listing.

They should sound like the next natural step in a social experience.

---

## 9. Cards and panels

Cards and panels are the main visual building blocks of this system.

### Card feel

Cards should feel:

- soft at the edges
- layered but not busy
- denser than before, but still breathable
- useful before decorative

### Panel feel

Panels inside cards or pages should support clarity, not create visual clutter.

The system should avoid:

- too many nested shells
- too many borders
- a dashboard feel

The card language should make the page feel curated rather than assembled from generic blocks.

---

## 10. Interaction language

The motion and interaction language should feel like a soft continuation of the visual language.

### Desired interaction feel

- hover should feel like a gentle lift, not a bounce
- press should feel like a quiet confirmation, not a hard click
- focus should be visible and respectful
- open / close transitions should feel orienting, not flashy

### What motion should do

Motion should help the user feel:

- where they are
- what just changed
- what the next step is

Motion should not be used just to decorate.

---

## 11. Responsive feel

The same emotional system should hold across screen sizes.

### Mobile

Mobile should feel:

- compact
- thumb-friendly
- calm
- readable in one glance
- generous enough to breathe, but tight enough to scan

### Desktop

Desktop should feel:

- broader
- calmer
- more comparative
- more layered

Desktop should not become a different product. It should just give the same system more room.

---

## 12. What to exclude

To keep the experience coherent, we should explicitly avoid:

- marketplace styling
- aggressive filtering
- cluttered faceted controls
- default button language
- overly playful visuals
- flat generic page sections
- severe enterprise styling
- map-first browsing as the main participant contract

This pass is about coherence, not adding more surface area.

---

## 13. Suggested order of work

This is the recommended sequence for the next design work.

### Step 1 - Lock the participant visual language

Decide the shared feel of:

- softness
- depth
- spacing
- corners
- motion
- button tone

### Step 2 - Apply it to the most visible surfaces

Bring that language into:

- browse cards
- primary buttons
- screen headers
- detail page structure
- apply page structure

### Step 3 - Refine the interactions

Tune:

- hover
- press
- focus
- transitions
- screen-to-screen continuity

This order matters because the product has to feel like one system before it can feel polished.

---

## 14. Success criteria

This pass is successful when a participant can move through the journey and feel:

- the screens belong together
- the buttons belong to the same world as the cards
- the page feels warm and premium without feeling busy
- the inspirations show up as product behavior, not just visual references
- the experience feels calm, social, and deliberate from browse to apply

---

## 15. Relationship to the existing browse spec

This design pass sits above the earlier `/events` dense browse spec.

The browse spec defines the shelf.
This spec defines the visual system around the shelf.

That means:

- the browse spec keeps the inventory contract
- this spec keeps the emotional and interaction contract
- both are needed for the full participant experience to feel complete
